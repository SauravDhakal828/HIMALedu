4. Humility Notes

In the essay "Humility," historian and philosopher Yuval Noah Harari explores the concept of humility not just as a personal virtue, but as a critical quality for societies and civilizations. He begins by challenging the widespread assumption that one's own culture, religion, or nation is the most important or superior. Harari argues that true humility means recognizing that no culture, including one’s own, holds a monopoly on truth or progress. This form of humility is essential for fostering peace, understanding, and cooperation in a diverse world.

Harari gives historical examples to explain how civilizations have often lacked humility. He talks about how European nations in the past believed they were the most advanced, using this belief to justify colonizing others. Similarly, many people today still assume their religion or nation is the center of history and the only one that matters. Harari criticizes this mindset, stating that such arrogance leads to conflict, intolerance, and a distorted view of history. He urges readers to reflect on how their own society might be making the same mistakes.

He then presents a more global and balanced view of history. For example, Harari reminds us that many important inventions and ideas—like the number zero or the concept of universities—originated not from Europe, but from other parts of the world like India and the Middle East. This perspective shows that human progress is a shared achievement, and every culture has contributed something valuable. Recognizing this truth is a sign of intellectual humility, which is necessary for a more accurate and inclusive understanding of the world.

Harari emphasizes that humility is not about putting oneself down or believing that one's culture has no value. Rather, it is about being open-minded, curious, and willing to learn from others. He suggests that a humble person—and a humble society—does not claim to know everything, nor does it try to dominate others. Instead, humility helps people understand that they are part of a larger human story and that their views might be limited or biased. This attitude fosters dialogue, learning, and mutual respect.

Towards the end of the essay, Harari points out that modern education often fails to teach humility. Students are taught to feel proud of their own nation's achievements but are rarely taught to appreciate what other cultures have given the world. He calls for a change in how we educate ourselves and others—to focus more on shared human history rather than national pride alone. Only by embracing humility can we avoid repeating the mistakes of the past and move towards a more united and peaceful global society.

In conclusion, “Humility” by Yuval Noah Harari is a powerful reflection on the need for self-awareness, openness, and respect for other cultures. It reminds us that no civilization is perfect or superior and that the world becomes better when we learn from one another. Harari’s message is especially relevant today, in an age of global challenges that require cooperation, understanding, and, most importantly, humility.

Understanding the text

a. Describe the claim of the Chinese nationalists about human history.
The Chinese nationalists claim that human history began with the Yellow Emperor and the Xia and Shang dynasties. They believe that everything significant achieved by other civilizations, such as Westerners, Muslims, or Indians, is essentially an imitation or continuation of Chinese accomplishments. In other words, they view China as the original source of all great historical progress.

b. What do pious Muslims believe about human history?
Pious Muslims believe that human history follows the teachings and guidance of the Quran. They view history as a divine narrative directed by Allah, where events unfold according to His will and the moral lessons contained in the Quran. Thus, their understanding of history is closely linked to their religious faith and the Quranic worldview.

c. What did the Aztecs firmly believe about the universe?
The Aztecs firmly believed that the universe is governed by cyclical patterns of creation and destruction. They thought that the cosmos undergoes repeated cycles of birth, death, and rebirth. According to their belief, the world was created and destroyed several times before the present era, emphasizing the transient and ever-changing nature of existence.

d. What, according to the essay, are the universal human abilities?
According to the essay, the universal human abilities are the capacity to learn, to create, and to understand. These abilities are shared by all humans across cultures and eras. No matter the differences in language, culture, or beliefs, every human being possesses the potential to gain knowledge, invent or make things, and comprehend the world around them.

e. How are the basic yoga postures derived from the shape of the letters of the Hebrew alphabet?
The essay explains that the basic yoga postures are connected symbolically to the shapes of the Hebrew alphabet letters. Each yoga posture reflects the form or spirit of a particular Hebrew letter, indicating that the physical movements in yoga are not just exercises but have a deeper, symbolic meaning linked to ancient spiritual traditions. This shows the interconnectedness of physical practice and language or spiritual symbolism.

Reference to the context

a. How do Hindu nationalists refute the Chinese claim that human history began with the Yellow Emperor and the Xia and Shang dynasties? Who do you agree with, and why?
Hindu nationalists strongly refute the Chinese nationalist claim by emphasizing the ancient origins of Indian civilization, particularly pointing to the Vedic culture as one of the oldest in the world. They argue that India’s historical and cultural contributions, such as the development of early sciences, philosophies, and spiritual knowledge, predate or at least parallel those of China. Hindu nationalists believe that many innovations and cultural elements attributed to China actually originated in India, and therefore, human history should not be seen through the lens of one single civilization but as a diverse tapestry with India playing a central role. This counterclaim is often rooted in pride for India’s rich heritage and a desire to reclaim a respected place in the global historical narrative.

However, agreeing strictly with one side overlooks the interconnectedness and complexity of human history. Civilizations across the world developed independently yet simultaneously, influencing each other through trade, migration, and cultural exchange. Instead of viewing history as a competition where one culture’s achievements negate another’s, it is more accurate and respectful to appreciate that human civilization is the result of multiple cultures contributing uniquely. Therefore, I believe neither the Chinese nor the Hindu nationalist claims fully capture the truth; instead, a more inclusive view acknowledges that history belongs to all humanity and that no single culture holds an exclusive claim to its beginning.

b. The author has dealt with a controversial debate on human history. Why do you think history has been a major contested issue in the present world?
History is a major contested issue today because it plays a crucial role in shaping collective identities, national pride, and political legitimacy. Different countries and cultural groups want to emphasize their historical achievements to bolster their self-esteem and justify their current social, political, or economic status. This competition to claim superiority in history often leads to disputes over historical facts, interpretations, and narratives. History textbooks, monuments, and public commemorations become arenas where these debates play out, as they influence how future generations perceive their place in the world. As a result, history is no longer just about understanding the past but is deeply intertwined with present-day power struggles and identity politics.

Furthermore, history is also contested because it influences international relations and cultural diplomacy. When nations disagree on historical interpretations, it can affect cooperation and trust, sometimes leading to tensions or conflicts. Additionally, groups marginalized or oppressed in history seek recognition and justice, which often means challenging dominant historical narratives. This dynamic makes history a living, evolving discourse rather than a fixed record. The author’s discussion highlights how history’s contested nature reflects ongoing struggles over memory, identity, and the meaning of civilization itself, making it one of the most sensitive and important debates in the modern world.
