7. A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings Notes

“A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings” is a magical realist short story by Gabriel García Márquez that explores human nature, compassion, and the clash between the extraordinary and the mundane. The story begins when a very old man with enormous wings mysteriously appears in a small village, astonishing the villagers and challenging their beliefs. The old man’s appearance raises questions about faith, cruelty, and human behavior.

The villagers are both fascinated and confused by the old man. They do not know whether he is an angel, a human, or some kind of supernatural being. Despite his wings, the old man is frail, dirty, and weak, which contrasts sharply with the villagers’ expectations of a divine creature. Instead of treating him with reverence, many villagers are skeptical, cruel, or exploitative. They cage him and show him off like a circus attraction, hoping to benefit from the curiosity he generates.

Throughout the story, Márquez uses magical realism to blend the ordinary with the fantastic. The old man’s wings symbolize mystery and the possibility of miracles, yet his physical condition reminds readers of human vulnerability. The villagers’ reactions reveal a mix of fear, ignorance, and selfishness. They fail to recognize the old man’s true nature and instead judge him by appearances and their own superstitions.

The story also highlights themes of compassion and cruelty. While some characters, like the child and Pelayo’s wife, show moments of kindness and curiosity, the majority treat the old man harshly or with indifference. This contrast exposes the complexity of human nature—our capacity for empathy alongside greed and cruelty. The story questions how society responds to the unfamiliar and the extraordinary.

In the end, the old man remains a mystery, eventually flying away as mysteriously as he came. His departure leaves the villagers to reflect on their own behavior and beliefs, but the story does not provide clear answers. Márquez invites readers to think about faith, human nature, and the way we treat those who are different or misunderstood.

In conclusion, “A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings” is a rich and thought-provoking story that blends fantasy and reality to explore deep human themes. Gabriel García Márquez uses magical realism to challenge readers to look beyond appearances and to question how we respond to the miraculous and the unknown in our lives.

Understanding the text

a. How does the narrator describe the weather and its effects in the exposition of the story?
The narrator describes the weather as continuous rain falling for three days, filling the land and houses with water. The sky and sea turn an ash grey color, and the shore’s sand glimmers faintly like powdered light but is actually a mix of mud and rotten shellfish. The light is weak and hard to see, setting a gloomy and oppressive atmosphere at the start.

b. Describe the strange old man as Pelayo and his wife first encounter within their courtyard.
Pelayo and his wife find the strange old man lying face down in the mud. He looks like a rag picker, with a bald head and almost completely shaved. His mouth is nearly toothless, and he has huge, dirty buzzard wings that are half-plucked and muddy. His overall appearance is decrepit and unusual.

c. Why did Pelayo and Elisenda imprison the old man in the chicken coop?
They imprisoned the old man in the chicken coop because they thought he might be an angel sent to take their sick child. They wanted to keep him confined and safe while they figured out what to do.

d. Why was Father Gonzaga not sure about the old man being a celestial messenger?
Father Gonzaga doubted the old man was an angel because he didn’t know how to greet ministers properly and couldn’t understand the language of God. When Father Gonzaga greeted him in Latin, the old man only murmured something in an unknown dialect. Also, the old man’s appearance was too human and lacked the dignity expected of angels.

e. Many people gathered at Pelayo’s house to see the strange old man. Why do you think the crowd assembled to see him?
The crowd gathered because the old man was very unusual and mysterious, especially due to his enormous wings. People were curious and fascinated, some thinking he might be an angel or a celestial messenger, while others came for entertainment or to make fun of him.

f. Some miracles happened while the crowd gathers to see the strange man. What are these miracles?
Some miracles mentioned include a blind man who could not regain his sight but grew three new teeth, a paralytic man who could not walk but almost won the lottery, and a leper whose infected sores grew sunflowers instead.

g. State the irritating things that the people did with the strange old man.
The crowd was often cruel and teasing toward the old man. They threw stones at him, burned his side with an iron rod causing injury, pulled his feathers, and treated him like an animal rather than a human or divine being.

h. How and why was the woman changed into a spider?
The woman was changed into a spider as punishment for disobeying her parents. She went out to dance at night without permission, and on her way back, a massive lightning strike hit the sky. A bolt of brimstone struck her and transformed her into a giant spider.

i. Describe how Elisenda saw the strange man flying over the houses?
Elisenda saw the old man flying while she was chopping vegetables in the kitchen. She felt a breeze, went to the window, and saw the old man struggling to fly. After some effort, he successfully flew away over the houses and disappeared from sight.

Reference to text

a. The arrival of a strange old man at Pelayo’s courtyard arouses many suspicions and explanations. Explain how the neighbour woman, Father Gonzaga and the doctor speak of the strange man. Why do you think these three people give three different kinds of interpretations?
When the strange old man with enormous wings arrives at Pelayo’s courtyard, different people respond to him in various ways based on their beliefs and roles. The neighbor woman immediately calls him an “angel” who has come to take their sick child. She explains his weakened state by saying that the rain has knocked him down. Her interpretation is rooted in local folklore and superstition, reflecting how common people often explain mysterious events through religious or mythical ideas.

In contrast, Father Gonzaga, the local priest, is doubtful about the old man being a celestial messenger. He notes that the old man does not understand Latin, the language of the Church, and fails to greet ministers properly. Additionally, the old man’s dirty appearance and the parasites on his wings do not fit the dignified image of angels as described by the Church. Father Gonzaga’s view is shaped by religious doctrine and a need for clear signs before accepting anything supernatural.

The doctor, on the other hand, approaches the strange old man from a scientific perspective. After examining him, the doctor concludes that the old man is very old and sick, noticing unusual sounds in his heart and kidneys. He treats the old man as a biological being with a strange condition rather than a supernatural entity.

These three different interpretations arise because each person sees the strange old man through their own worldview. The neighbor woman’s perspective comes from popular belief, Father Gonzaga’s from organized religion, and the doctor’s from science and rationalism. This variety highlights how people try to understand the unknown in ways that align with their cultural, religious, or scientific backgrounds.

b. This story belongs to the genre of ‘magical realism, a genre perfected by Gabriel Garcia Marquez in his novels and short stories. Magical realism is a narrative technique in which the storyteller narrates the commonplace things with magical colour and the events look both magical and real at the same time. Collect five magic realist happenings from the story and argue why they seem magical to you.
The story A Very Old Man with Enormous Wings is a fine example of magical realism, where ordinary events are narrated with magical elements, blending the real and the fantastical seamlessly.

1. The very presence of the old man with enormous wings: This is the most striking magical element. He looks human yet possesses large, dirty, buzzard-like wings, which is an unusual and mysterious blending of human and mythical traits in an otherwise ordinary setting. 2. The old man's incomprehensible language: His inability to communicate in any known human language, only murmuring in an "unknown dialect," suggests he is from another realm, adding a layer of mystery beyond normal human experience. 3. The transformation of the woman into a giant spider: This bizarre and sudden metamorphosis, described as a punishment for disobedience, introduces a surreal and grotesque element that defies natural laws, reinforcing the magical tone. 4. The "miracles" that happen around the old man: The blind man growing three new teeth (but not regaining sight), the paralytic almost winning the lottery (but not walking), and the leper's sores blooming into sunflowers are strange, symbolic, and subtly magical occurrences that twist conventional expectations of miracles. 5. The old man's eventual flight: Despite his initial frailty and the mundane conditions of his captivity, his ability to eventually grow new feathers and fly away, struggling but ultimately disappearing over the horizon, is a magical image that contrasts sharply with the harsh reality and indifference he faced.

These events seem magical because they break the rules of ordinary life and natural possibility, yet the story presents them as part of normal, everyday life without extensive explanation or wonder from the characters. This seamless integration of the fantastical into the mundane is the hallmark of magical realism.

c. The author introduces the episode of a woman who became a spider for having disobeyed her parents. This episode at once shifts people’s concentration from the strange old man to the spider woman. What do you think is the purpose of the author to bring this shift in the story?
The author introduces the episode of the woman who is transformed into a giant spider as a punishment for disobeying her parents, which immediately shifts the attention of the villagers from the strange old man with enormous wings to this new, bizarre spectacle. This shift highlights how easily people’s interests change, especially when something fresh and sensational appears. The villagers, who were initially fascinated by the old man’s mysterious presence, quickly lose interest in him as soon as the spider woman arrives. This shows human nature’s fickleness and short attention span, revealing how people are often distracted by novelty and fail to sustain their curiosity or concern over time. The episode also strengthens the story’s magical realism by introducing another fantastical element that seems just as strange and unbelievable as the old man, blending the magical and real worlds.

Furthermore, the shift serves as a critique of human greed, cruelty, and superficiality. Pelayo and Elisenda exploit the old man by charging admission to see him, benefiting financially from people’s curiosity. However, when the spider woman appears, the villagers forget about the old man and no longer care for him, allowing him to suffer neglect and mistreatment. This contrast exposes the selfishness of people who use the unfamiliar for entertainment or profit but lack true empathy or understanding. By diverting attention away from the old man, the author comments on society’s tendency to overlook what is genuinely extraordinary or meaningful, focusing instead on trivial distractions. This episode deepens the story’s message about human nature and the complex ways people respond to the magical and mysterious in everyday life.

d. The story deals with the common people’s gullibility. How do Pelayo and his wife take advantage of common people’s whom?
The story highlights the gullibility of common people and how Pelayo and Elisenda take advantage of this trait for their own benefit. When the strange old man with wings appears in their courtyard, many villagers become curious and flock to see him, believing he might be an angel or a celestial messenger. Pelayo and Elisenda quickly realize that people’s fascination with the old man can be turned into a source of income. They start charging five cents as admission for people to come and see the “angel.” This small fee soon brings them a lot of money, which they use to improve their living conditions and eventually build a luxurious house.

Pelayo and Elisenda’s actions reveal how they exploit the common people’s superstitions and willingness to believe in the extraordinary without question. Instead of caring for the old man or understanding him, they treat him as a means to gain wealth. Their greed blinds them to the old man’s suffering and dignity. This manipulation of the villagers’ curiosity and beliefs reflects a broader commentary on human nature—how people can be easily fooled and how others may take advantage of that gullibility for personal gain.
