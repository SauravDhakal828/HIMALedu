5. Soft Storm Notes

“Soft Storm” by Derek Walcott is a beautifully crafted poem that captures the quiet and subtle intensity of a natural storm. Unlike typical storms that are violent and destructive, this poem presents a storm that is gentle yet powerful in its own way. Through rich imagery and vivid descriptions, Walcott explores the interplay between nature’s calmness and its latent strength, inviting readers to appreciate the delicate balance of forces in the environment.

The poem begins by describing the storm as soft and subtle, using words that evoke a sense of gentleness rather than aggression. This soft storm does not roar or rage loudly but moves quietly, almost like a whisper. Walcott’s choice of language reflects the understated power of nature, showing that even calmness can carry energy and influence. This portrayal challenges common perceptions of storms and emphasizes the varied moods of the natural world.

As the poem progresses, the imagery becomes more vivid, painting pictures of the storm’s effect on the surroundings. The trees sway gently, leaves flutter, and the air is filled with a soft rustling sound. The storm, though not violent, creates a dynamic and lively atmosphere, showing that nature’s power is not always about noise and destruction. Walcott highlights the beauty in this quiet motion, encouraging readers to notice the smaller, often overlooked details of the natural world.

The poem also touches upon the theme of change and transformation. The soft storm symbolizes a gentle shift, a moment of transition in the environment. This movement suggests that change can be calm and natural rather than abrupt and harsh. Walcott’s depiction of the storm as a soft, transformative force invites reflection on how subtle changes in life and nature can have profound effects.

In the concluding lines, the poem suggests a harmony between the storm and its surroundings. The storm does not dominate or destroy but becomes a part of the landscape’s rhythm. This integration conveys a message about coexistence and balance in nature. The poem leaves readers with a sense of peace and appreciation for the quiet powers that shape our world.

In conclusion, “Soft Storm” is a lyrical and thoughtful poem that celebrates the gentle yet potent forces of nature. Derek Walcott’s use of soft, evocative imagery invites readers to reconsider their ideas about strength and power, recognizing that sometimes, subtlety can be just as impactful as force. The poem is a tribute to the quiet beauty and transformative energy present in the natural world.

Understanding the Text

a. When does the speaker grow soft? Enlist the occasions when he grows soft.
The speaker grows soft when he feels compassion and sorrow about the suffering and turmoil around him. Specifically, he grows soft:

  • When he hears the tumult of the earth.
  • When the sky looks like crocuses blooming.
  • When the moon skids down.
  • When the moon sings of lampposts and gutters in the seamless city.

b. What do you understand by ‘this seamless city’?
‘This seamless city’ refers to a city that appears to be smooth and without disruptions or boundaries. It symbolizes a place free from visible conflicts or breaks, representing freedom and fluidity in human life and choices.

c. Describe the poor children portrayed in the poem.
The poor children in the poem are homeless and hungry, found crying under a tree in the streets of Thamel. They represent the neglected and suffering section of society, emphasizing poverty and despair amid the city’s hustle.

d. What do you understand by ‘the unwedded gardens of history’?
‘The unwedded gardens of history’ symbolize the neglected culture and history of the Kathmandu Valley. It suggests that the rich heritage has been left unprotected and ignored for a long time.

e. Why was the forlorn child wailing?
The forlorn child was wailing because he was searching for his mother amidst the violent and troubled history, symbolizing loss, separation, and the harsh realities of life.

f. What do you understand about ‘soft storm’?
‘Soft storm’ is a paradoxical expression where ‘storm’ typically means violence or turmoil, but ‘soft’ suggests gentleness. In the poem, it represents the speaker’s inner emotional disturbance—a storm of feelings that is painful yet not destructive.

g. Why does the speaker call our time ‘mad time’?
The speaker calls it ‘mad time’ because he sees the world filled with chaos, destruction, and irrational behavior. People behave selfishly, unlawfully, and forget humanity and compassion, leading to a society full of disorder and confusion.

h. What does the speaker want to do in “hard times”?
In hard times, the speaker wishes to “melt like a rainbow,” which means he wants to remain gentle, hopeful, and beautiful despite the harsh and difficult circumstances.

Reference to the context:

a. The poet uses the word ‘soft’ with the words like ‘storm’ and ‘gale’, which generally refer to disorder and violence. What effect does the poet achieve through the use of such anomalous expressions?
The use of the word ‘soft’ with words like ‘storm’ and ‘gale’ is an example of an anomalous or paradoxical expression because ‘storm’ and ‘gale’ usually suggest harshness, violence, and chaos, while ‘soft’ implies gentleness and calmness. By combining these contradictory ideas, the poet creates a powerful effect that reflects the complex emotions inside the speaker.

This paradoxical use helps to express the speaker’s inner turmoil and emotional disturbance that is intense but not outwardly destructive. It conveys a feeling of sadness and compassion that quietly affects the speaker rather than causing outward violence. The ‘soft storm’ represents the subtle, gentle, yet deeply unsettling feelings that arise when the speaker witnesses the sufferings and chaos around him. Through this unusual pairing, the poet also captures the contradictions of modern life — the coexistence of beauty and pain, calm and chaos, compassion and cruelty. It makes the reader think more deeply about the nature of emotions and the quiet struggles people face in turbulent times.

In short, the anomalous expression emphasizes the speaker’s psychological state and helps convey the poem’s theme of emotional sensitivity amid societal disorder.

b. What is the speaker’s attitude towards the time he describes in the poem?
The speaker’s attitude towards the time he describes in the poem is one of deep concern and sadness. He refers to it as a “mad time,” indicating that the era is filled with chaos, irrationality, and disorder. The speaker observes many unnatural and disturbing phenomena around him, such as stones growing on flowers and rhododendrons blooming in winter, which symbolize the confusion and disruption of natural and social order. This “madness” is reflected in people’s behavior as well — selfishness, greed, lawlessness, and a disregard for human values have become widespread. The speaker feels disturbed by the collapse of moral standards and the loss of mutual respect and compassion among people. This critical view shows that he is deeply troubled by the state of society and its direction.

Furthermore, the speaker’s attitude is not just one of criticism but also of sorrow and empathy. While he is aware of the corruption and disorder, he also feels a compassionate sadness for the victims of these “hard times.” His emotions are complex, as he struggles to reconcile the beauty of life with the harsh realities around him. The phrase “soft storm” itself reflects this inner turmoil — a gentle but persistent feeling of unrest and pain. Thus, the speaker’s attitude combines a critical awareness of societal madness with a sensitive emotional response to the suffering and confusion that define his time.

c. What is the speaker like? Is he a rebel? Why? Why not?
The speaker in the poem “Soft Storm” is a sensitive and compassionate observer of the world around him. He is deeply disturbed by the chaos, violence, and injustice he witnesses in society. Rather than remaining indifferent, he feels the pain and suffering of others, especially the poor and the marginalized. His emotional softness, symbolized by the “soft storm,” reflects his inner turmoil and compassion. He is thoughtful and reflective, trying to make sense of the “mad time” in which he lives. The speaker’s sensitivity shows his humane nature and a desire for understanding and change.

In some ways, the speaker can be seen as a rebel, though his rebellion is subtle and indirect. He challenges the disorder and selfishness of modern society by expressing his sorrow and disapproval through poetry. He does not openly fight or protest but uses his words to highlight the problems and the loss of humanity he perceives. This quiet resistance against corruption, cruelty, and indifference is a form of rebellion rooted in empathy and moral concern. However, since he does not take aggressive or overt actions, his rebellion remains internal and poetic rather than active or revolutionary.

d. Explain the stanza below in your own words:
I became soft
when I saw
a blood-stained shirt
speaking in the earth’s ears
with bruised human lips
in the far corner
under the moon
of history and dreams
playing hide and seek
in open museums
of human times.

In this stanza, the speaker reveals how he became emotionally soft and compassionate when he witnessed a blood-stained shirt lying on the ground. The shirt, marked by blood, seems to silently communicate its pain and suffering to the earth, almost as if it could tell a story of violence and human cruelty. The reference to “bruised human lips” suggests the presence of a wounded or suffering person connected to the shirt, symbolizing the harsh realities and scars left by conflict or hardship. This powerful image evokes a sense of helplessness and sorrow, showing how deeply the speaker is affected by what he sees.

The setting of the scene is described as being “under the moon of history and dreams,” which means the pain and suffering are not isolated incidents but are part of a larger, ongoing historical context. The moon symbolizes time and memory, shining its light on the past and present, where history and human aspirations coexist. The phrase “playing hide and seek in open museums of human times” suggests that these memories and stories of human experiences — both joyful and tragic — are exposed for everyone to witness, like exhibits in a museum. This highlights the contrast between the beauty of dreams and the harshness of reality. Overall, the stanza conveys the speaker’s deep empathy and sorrow, showing how the suffering of others touches him profoundly and makes him feel vulnerable and tender inside.
