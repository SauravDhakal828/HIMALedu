6. The Half-closed Eyes of the Buddha and the Slowly Sinking Sun Notes

This poem/story by Sudama Pandey ‘Dhoomil’ combines vivid imagery with deep philosophical reflection, exploring themes of awareness, detachment, and the passage of time. The title itself symbolizes two powerful images: the Buddha’s half-closed eyes representing calm detachment and spiritual insight, and the slowly sinking sun symbolizing the inevitable movement toward the end, change, and impermanence.

The poem begins with the image of the Buddha’s half-closed eyes. This image conveys a sense of inner peace and serene awareness. Unlike eyes wide open, which represent alertness to the outer world, the Buddha’s half-closed eyes suggest a meditative state, where one is inwardly focused and detached from worldly distractions. It teaches the reader about the importance of maintaining calmness and a balanced perspective in the midst of life’s chaos.

Next, the slowly sinking sun serves as a metaphor for the gradual passage of time and the approach of life’s end. The poem reflects on how, like the sun setting at the end of the day, human life moves steadily towards closure. This image evokes a mixture of sadness and acceptance, reminding readers that all things are transient and that nothing in life lasts forever. It encourages an acceptance of change rather than resistance to it.

Throughout the poem, there is a contemplative tone that urges readers to embrace both detachment and awareness. The Buddha’s eyes symbolize spiritual wisdom and the calm acceptance of life’s impermanence, while the sinking sun reminds us to live mindfully and appreciate the present moment. The poem blends these images to suggest that true peace comes from understanding the transient nature of existence and maintaining equanimity.

In conclusion, “The Half-closed Eyes of the Buddha and the Slowly Sinking Sun” is a profound work that combines spiritual reflection with natural imagery. Sudama Pandey ‘Dhoomil’ encourages readers to cultivate inner peace by adopting detachment like the Buddha and to face life’s inevitable changes with grace, much like watching the sun slowly set. The poem serves as a meditation on how awareness and acceptance can bring tranquility in an ever-changing world.

Understanding the Text

a. How does the tourist describe his initial impression of the Kathmandu valley?
The tourist describes the Kathmandu valley with admiration for its natural beauty—the serene mountains, colorful clay homes painted red, yellow, and white, and the fresh fragrance of the soil. She is captivated by the peaceful atmosphere and scenic landscape that fill the air with tranquility.

b. According to the tourist, why is the West indebted to the East?
The tourist believes the West is indebted to the East because the East has contributed significantly to world civilization through ancient scriptures like the Puranas, intricate brass figures, ivory decorations, palm leaf manuscripts, and copperplate inscriptions. These cultural, religious, and artistic heritages from the East have deeply influenced the West.

c. How does the tourist interpret the gaze of the monks and nuns?
The tourist interprets the gaze of the monks and nuns as the ‘samyak gaze,’ meaning a pure, uncontaminated, and holy sight free from prejudice or discrimination. Their eyes reflect spiritual calmness and a deep sense of peace.

d. Why do the tourists think Nepali people are wonderful and exceptional?
Tourists consider Nepali people wonderful and exceptional because of their peaceful coexistence despite cultural and religious diversity. The harmonious life they lead together reflects tolerance and mutual respect.

e. What are the different kinds of communities in the Kathmandu valley and how do they coexist with each other?
The Kathmandu valley is home to various communities including Aryans, non-Aryans, Hindus, and Buddhists. They coexist peacefully because the shared Nepali soil fosters unity, allowing them to grow and live harmoniously despite their differences.

f. What does the tourist feel about the temple of Adinath?
The tourist views the Adinath temple as a living symbol of Nepalese tolerance and harmony. It stands out as a testament to the coexistence of multiple gods, religions, and philosophies in Nepal, reflecting the country's spirit of unity.

g. Why does the guide take the tourist to the remote village?
The guide takes the tourist to a remote village to reveal the harsh realities behind the valley’s beauty—showing the poverty, suffering, and sympathetic struggles of people that are usually unseen by tourists and unrecorded in books or photos.

h. What does the innocent village couple think of the doctor?
The innocent village couple regards the doctor as their eldest son who has returned from across the seas with life-saving treatment for their paralyzed child, symbolizing hope and healing.

i. What are the differences between the paralyzed child and his sister?
The paralyzed boy is severely disabled—unable to speak, move his hands, chew food, or respond to commands, with only his eyes indicating life. In contrast, his sister is healthy, crawling around, exploring her surroundings actively.

j. Why does the guide show the instances of poverty to the tourist?
The guide wants the tourist to see beyond the valley’s surface beauty and cultural richness to understand the real struggles faced by ordinary people—the poverty and hardship hidden behind the picturesque landscape—to give a fuller, more truthful perspective of Nepal.

Reference to the Context

a. Which narrative technique is used by the author to tell the story? How is this story different from other stories you have read?
The author uses the stream of consciousness narrative technique to tell the story. This technique presents the thoughts and feelings of the characters in a continuous flow, allowing readers to experience the story through the inner reflections and perspectives of the tourist and the guide.

This story is different from many others because it shows two contrasting viewpoints simultaneously—the tourist’s idealized, picturesque view of Kathmandu and Nepal, and the guide’s realistic, often harsh portrayal of poverty and struggle. Unlike conventional stories that usually follow a single perspective or straightforward plot, this story offers a dual narrative that captures both beauty and reality, blending them to provide a deeper understanding of the place and its people.

b. How is the author able to integrate two fragments of the narration into a unified whole?
The author integrates the two fragments of narration—the tourist’s idealized view and the guide’s realistic perspective—by presenting them as contrasting yet complementary sides of the same story. The tourist sees the Kathmandu valley through the lens of beauty, culture, and spirituality, admiring the serene mountains, colorful homes, and peaceful coexistence of diverse communities. This part of the narration highlights Nepal’s rich traditions, harmony, and natural charm. In contrast, the guide reveals the hidden realities of poverty, suffering, and hardship that are often overlooked by visitors. By showing the harsh conditions faced by villagers, the guide brings depth and balance to the narrative, ensuring that the story does not remain superficial.

By weaving these two perspectives together, the author creates a unified whole that captures both the external allure and the internal struggles of Nepal. This technique encourages readers to see beyond surface appearances and understand the complexity of life in Kathmandu valley. The interplay between the tourist’s admiration and the guide’s sobering truths highlights the need for empathy and a deeper awareness of reality. In doing so, the author successfully combines two viewpoints to offer a richer, more authentic portrayal of Nepal’s culture, people, and challenges.

c. The author brings some historical and legendary references to the story. Collect these references and show their significance in the story.
The story “Half-closed Eyes of Buddha” incorporates several historical and legendary references that add depth and cultural significance to the narrative. One of the most prominent references is the legend of Manjushri, a Bodhisattva who is said to have cut a gorge at Chobhar to drain the ancient lake that once covered the Kathmandu valley, thereby making the land habitable. This legend not only explains the geographical formation of the valley but also symbolizes the connection between nature and spirituality in Nepalese culture. It highlights how the valley itself is born out of a divine act, imbuing the landscape with sacredness and linking the people to their rich mythological past. This reference roots the story in Nepal’s historical and spiritual heritage, reminding readers that the land and its culture are inseparable from the legends that define them.

In addition to this, the author brings attention to Nepal’s immense contribution to world civilization, especially the East’s influence on the West. References to Puranas, brass figures, ivory decorations, palm leaf manuscripts, and copperplate inscriptions emphasize the depth of Nepalese and Eastern cultural, religious, and artistic heritage. These elements symbolize the richness of tradition that has been passed down through generations, showing Nepal as a repository of ancient wisdom and art. Furthermore, the samyak gaze of the monks and nuns represents spiritual purity and harmony, reflecting the peaceful coexistence of diverse religions and philosophies in Nepal. Finally, the half-closed eyes of the Buddha paired with the setting sun symbolize tranquility, tolerance, and unity, underscoring the story’s overarching message about Nepal’s peaceful and harmonious society. These historical and legendary references collectively enrich the story by blending mythology, culture, and spirituality, making the narrative not just a description of a place but a reflection on its timeless values.

d. The author talks about the eyes in many places: the eyes of the shaven monks and nuns, eyes in the window and door panels, the eyes of the Himalayas, the eyes of the paralyzed boy, the eyes of the welcoming villagers and above all the half-closed eyes of the Buddha. Explain how all the instances of eyes contribute to the overall unity of the story.
The author uses the motif of “eyes” in various forms throughout the story to create a symbolic thread that unites different aspects of Nepal’s culture, spirituality, and reality. The eyes of the shaven monks and nuns, described as the ‘samyak gaze,’ represent purity, spiritual awareness, and an unbiased vision that sees beyond superficial differences. These eyes symbolize a higher level of consciousness and peace, reflecting the deep-rooted religious harmony in Nepal. Similarly, the eyes in the window and door panels and the eyes of the Himalayas serve as silent witnesses to the lives and traditions of the people. They represent the ever-watchful presence of nature and history that observe the unfolding human stories in the valley, linking the physical environment to the cultural identity of the place.

On a more personal and emotional level, the eyes of the paralyzed boy and the eyes of the welcoming villagers add a human touch to the story. The boy’s eyes, despite his paralysis, signify life, hope, and silent communication, while the villagers’ eyes reflect warmth, acceptance, and resilience amidst hardship. Above all, the half-closed eyes of the Buddha symbolize calmness, balance, and spiritual peace, serving as a metaphor for Nepal’s overarching spirit of tolerance and unity. By connecting all these “eyes,” the author weaves together the spiritual, cultural, natural, and human dimensions of the story, creating a cohesive narrative that celebrates Nepal’s rich heritage and the harmonious coexistence of its people and landscape.
